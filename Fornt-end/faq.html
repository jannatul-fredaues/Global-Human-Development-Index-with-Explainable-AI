The basics

Start here
HDI is a single number, between 0 and 1, that summarises how a country is doing across three dimensions: a long and healthy life, access to knowledge, and a decent standard of living. It was designed as an alternative to ranking countries by income alone — a high income doesn't always mean a high quality of life, and HDI tries to capture that gap.
Three sub-indices are built first — one each for health, education, and income — by normalising raw indicators like life expectancy, expected years of schooling, and gross national income per capita against fixed global minimums and maximums. The final HDI is then the geometric mean of those three indices, not a simple average, which means a country can't fully compensate for a weak index with a strong one elsewhere.
The indicator data mirrors the sources the UNDP itself draws on — bodies like the UN Population Division, UNESCO, and the World Bank. This platform doesn't collect its own survey data; it works from the same published statistics and focuses on making the resulting scores explainable.

The explainability layer

The "why" panel
It means every score comes with a breakdown of which of the three indices pulled it up or down, and by roughly how much. Instead of just showing you the final number, the panel runs the calculation in reverse so you can see the reasoning, not just the result.
The HDI formula itself is fixed and published by the UNDP — there's nothing to predict there. The explainability layer sits on top of it, attributing each score's movement back to its three inputs in a way that's transparent by construction, rather than a black-box model you have to trust blindly.

About the project

Context
This is a Final Year Project exploring how explainable AI techniques can be applied to a well-known, already-transparent index like HDI — using it as a clear test case before applying the same approach to less transparent models. See the About page for more on the team and motivation.
The underlying HDI figures are public statistics, so they're free to reference with the usual attribution to the original source. If you'd like to reuse the platform itself or its explainability approach, reach out via the About page first.
